Kinesin superfamily proteins play a fundamental role in intracellular transport such as axonal transport.1 Disturbed axonal transport has been associated with various human neurodegenerative diseases, such as amyotrophic lateral sclerosis, Alzheimer's disease, Charcot-Marie-Tooth peripheral neuropathy, and spastic paraplegia (SPG).1 Mutations in KIF5A (MIM: 602821), encoding kinesin heavy chain isoform 5A (KIF5A), have been associated with SPG10 (MIM: 604187).2 MAPK8IP3 (MIM: 605431) encodes c-Jun-amino-terminal kinase (JNK)-interacting protein 3 (JIP3), also known as JNK/stress-activated protein kinase-associated protein 1 (JSAP1). JIP3 is a scaffold protein for mitogen-activated protein kinase (MAPK) and an adaptor protein for the kinesin-1 complex, which comprises two KIF5s and two kinesin light chains.1 JIP3 homologs are essential for axonal transport in neurons, and JIP3 deficiency disturbs axonal transport, leading to neuronal degeneration in invertebrates and vertebrates, including rats,3, 4 mice,5, 6 zebrafish,7 fruit flies,8, 9 and Caenorhabditis elegans.10 However, an association between MAPK8IP3 variants and human disease has not been established, although a de novo variant in MAPK8IP3, p.(Glu455Gly), has been identified as a variant of uncertain significance combined with a variant in NDN (MIM: 602117) in an individual with Smith-Magenis–like syndrome.11 Additionally, another de novo variant, p.(Tyr94Cys), was found in a patient from a large cohort of patients with autism.12

Here, we report on two recurrent de novo missense variants in MAPK8IP3 in 5 individuals from four families with neurodevelopmental symptoms, including spastic diplegia, intellectual disability, and corpus callosum hypoplasia.

Materials and Methods

The present study was approved by the institutional review boards of Tohoku University School of Medicine, the National Center for Child Health and Development, and Yokohama City University. Written informed consent was obtained from all study participants. All zebrafish studies were approved by the Animal Committee of the Tohoku University School of Medicine.

Exome Sequencing

Exome sequencing was performed as previously reported13-15 in three institutions: Tohoku University, the National Center for Child Health and Development, and Yokohama City University.

Zebrafish Study

The zebrafish SAT line (Sanger AB Tübingen) was obtained from ZIRC (Zebrafish International Resource Center) and maintained as previously reported.16 Transient mosaic expression of human JIP3 and the variants was achieved by injecting the DNA expression construct, neurod:hJIP3 (wild type or variant)/mCherry, which was controlled by a neuron-specific promoter,7 into fertilized eggs. Of the injected larvae, approximately 1% showed a single posterior lateral line (pLL) neuron labeled with mCherry and were subjected to imaging analysis. Axon images were obtained at 36hpf using a confocal laser-scanning microscope (Zeiss LSM800; Carl Zeiss, Jena, Germany) by projecting a 3- to 8-μm Z-axis depth around the labeled pLL axon.

Identification of Two Recurrent De Novo Variants in MAPK8IP3

We identified two recurrent de novo MAPK8IP3 (GenBank: NM_015133.4) variants in all 5 patients from four unrelated families. A de novo variant, c.1732C>T (p.Arg578Cys), was found in 3 individuals, including 2 siblings (individuals 1 and 2), suggesting possible germline mosaicism in 1 of the parents. The other variant, c.3436C>T (p.Arg1146Cys), was found in 2 individuals. The two variants, p.Arg578Cys and p.Arg1146Cys, are absent in ExAC, gnomAD, Human Genetic Variation Database, and ToMMo 3.5KJPNv2. Both missense variants also affect evolutionarily conserved residues (Fig 1T) and are predicted to be pathogenic by different in silico prediction tools (SIFT, Polyphen-2, MutationTaster, and CADD). p.Arg1146Cys was also found in an individual with an anomaly of the nervous system and neck or head in the DDD (Deciphering Developmental Disorders) Study, although detailed clinical information was unavailable.

Zebrafish Study

The pLL nerve emerging from the ganglion beside the otocyst results in long axons along the mid-body line toward the tail of the zebrafish. We examined whether expression of JIP3 variants affects developing pLL axons, because an abnormality of the lateral line reportedly occurs in JIP3–/– zebrafish.7 A smooth long axon was observed when wild-type human JIP3 tagged with mCherry was transiently expressed in a single pLL neuron. When the variant JIP3R578C or JIP3R1146C was expressed in a pLL neuron, abnormal varicosities were exhibited in the axon (Fig 2).

Discussion

We report on two recurrent de novo missense variants in MAPK8IP3 in 5 individuals from four families with neurodevelopmental symptoms, including spastic diplegia, intellectual disability, and corpus callosum hypoplasia. Among the affected individuals with MAPK8IP3 variants, all except individual 4, who was the youngest affected individual, presented spastic diplegia. Individuals 4 and 5 presented with hypotonia in infancy. It is possible that individual 4 had simply not yet developed spastic diplegia. Another explanation for the presentation of individual 4 is that his low muscle tone obscured his spasticity. Motor developmental delay was noticed from infancy in all affected individuals, and no regression was observed.

In some affected individuals, some observations and data, including histopathological and electrophysiological findings, were consistent with neuropathy (Supplementary Table 1). Sensory impairment below the knees in individuals 1 and 2 was observed by the parents. In individuals 1 and 2, patellar tendon reflexes were exaggerated, but Achilles tendon reflexes were not. Sural nerve biopsy of individual 2 revealed loss of large myelinated fibers (Fig 1H). Electromyography of individual 2 revealed a neurogenic pattern. The motor nerve conduction study showed a reduction in velocity or a lack of response predominantly in the lower limbs of individuals 1 and 2. These data support the presence of axonal neuropathy at least in some affected individuals with MAPK8IP3 variants.

All affected individuals presented with severe intellectual disability. A potential difference may exist in verbal and nonverbal skills between the two genotypes. The 3 individuals with p.Arg578Cys understand language to some extent and can speak or express emotions, whereas the 2 individuals with p.Arg1146Cys have autistic behaviors and cannot speak. Individuals 2 and 3 have epilepsy (Supplementary Table 1). Brain MRI showed cerebral atrophy and a hypoplastic corpus callosum as well as reduced white matter volume in all 5 individuals (Fig 1I–R). Delayed myelination was observed in 3 individuals. The affected individuals were more likely to exhibit facial features, including a round face (particularly during childhood), thin upper lip, and prominent nasal bridge (Fig 1A–G). Interestingly, individual 5, with the p.Arg1146Cys variant, was suspected of having Smith-Magenis syndrome because her dysmorphic face resembled the features of Smith-Magenis syndrome, including long eyebrows, upslanting palpebral fissures, anteverted nares, short philtrum, and thin upper lip. Smith-Magenis–like features were previously reported in an individual with p.(Glu455Gly) in MAPK8IP3 as well as a variant in NDN.11

The two variants identified in this study are located at highly conserved residues and were absent in unaffected controls. In addition, these variants were predicted to affect residues within functional domains. p.Arg578 is predicted to be located in RILP homology 2 (RH2) domain (Fig 1S).17 The RH2 domain is reportedly involved in dimerization of proteins that have an RH2 domain and to interact with Rab36.18 In addition, JIP3 was predicted to have a WD40 repeat domain in the C-terminal region, including p.Arg1146, by Phyre2 (http://www.sbg.bio.ic.ac.uk/phyre2/; Fig 1U). Consistent with this finding, residues around p.Arg1146 were also predicted to have a WD40 repeat domain by another prediction tool, WDSP (http://wu.scbb.pkusz.edu.cn/wdsp/; Fig 1V). Interestingly, by WDSP, the p.Arg1146Cys variant was predicted to sit on a hotspot, which was thought to be a surface to interact with a binding partner. Finally, constraint data from the gnomAD database also revealed that missense variants are generally poorly tolerated in this gene (z = 3.04), as are loss-of-function variants (pLI = 1). Similarly, another recent study reported that the missense variant in MAPK8IP3 is a constraint (z = 4.42), and that MAPK8IP3 is included in a list of 1,003 highly constrained genes.19 Overall, these data support the pathogenicity of the two de novo variants.

The identification of these two recurrent de novo variants suggested that these variants did not occur by chance. We identified 4 unrelated individuals in a total of approximately 2,450 individuals in an undiagnosed exome cohort in our three institutions. The likelihood of de novo variants in the same residue, c.1732C>T (p.Arg578Cys) or c.3436C>T (p.Arg1146Cys), in MAPK8IP3 in 2 unrelated individuals was calculated to be 2.56 × 10–9, using the Poisson model as previously described.19, 20 This finding remained significant when we corrected for the 18,892 genes targeted in the Consensus Coding Sequence (CCDS release 20; p = 4.83 × 10–5). The likelihood that 4 unrelated individuals have de novo variants in either of the two residues in MAPK8IP3 was also very low (p = 3.30 × 10–13). Thus, the recurrent variants are likely to be associated with a neurodevelopmental disease rather than occurring by chance.

To test the pathogenicity of the two variants, we utilized a zebrafish model. When the variant JIP3R578C or JIP3R1146C was expressed in a pLL neuron, abnormal varicosities were exhibited in the axon, suggesting an adverse effect of these variants on the developing axons (Fig 2). Interestingly, similar varicosities of axons have also been reported in Jip3 knockout KO neurons in mice.6 The phenotype observed in the zebrafish axons expressing the JIP variants is in line with the observations of individuals with MAPK8IP3 variants, which appeared to affect long axons. Taken together, these data suggest that both missense variants cause defects in neural development by affecting JIP3 function.

Phenotype similarity between the zebrafish axons overexpressing the human mutant JIP3 and the axons of KO mice implies that both the variants are likely to act in a dominant-negative or toxic gain-of-function manner, rather than in haploinsufficiency. However, caution must be taken because the overexpressing mutant JIP3 might cause a nonspecific effect in the axons of zebrafish instead of showing a physiological effect compared with using the KO/knock-in technique. In addition, the possibility of the haploinsufficiency of MAPK8IP3 that causes some human disease phenotypes is not excluded, which is supported by constraint data from the gnomAD database.

In conclusion, we identified two recurrent de novo MAPK8IP3 variants in 5 individuals from four families with spastic diplegia, intellectual disability, cerebral atrophy, and corpus callosum hypoplasia. We showed that the two recurrent variants are significantly enriched, and that zebrafish embryos overexpressing human JIP3 with the two variants showed axon varicosities of the pLL nerve in zebrafish embryos at a higher rate than did wild-type zebrafish. These findings support the pathogenicity of the variants. Thus, we propose that MAPK8IP3 missense variants cause a human neurodevelopmental disease.
